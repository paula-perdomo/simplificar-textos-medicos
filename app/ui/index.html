Readability Score Interpretation

Readability formulas provide a numerical score to estimate the difficulty of a text. These scores are interpreted using two different systems: grade-level-based scores and the Flesch Reading Ease scale.

Interpretation for Grade-Level-Based Metrics

The following models output a score that directly estimates the U.S. grade level required to understand a text:

  • Coleman-Liau Index (CLI)
  • Gunning Fog Index (GFI)
  • SMOG Index
  • Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level (FKGL)
  • Dale-Chall Readability Score (DCRS)

The table below provides a general guide for interpreting this grade-level score.

Score ↓ School level Student age range Notes
0-1 Pre-kindergarten - 1st grade 3-7 Basic level for those who just learn to read books.
1-5 1st grade - 5th grade 7-11 Very easy to read.
5-8 5th grade - 8th grade 11-14 A text is considered ideal for average readers.
8-11 8th grade - 11th grade 14-17 Fairly difficult to read.
11 and above 11th grade - college 17 and above Too hard to read for the majority of readers.

Interpretation for Flesch Reading Ease (FRE)

Unlike the other metrics, the Flesch Reading Ease (FRE) operates on a 0–100 point scale. On this scale, a higher score indicates the text is easier to read. This score is then mapped to a corresponding readability level as follows:

FRE Score ↑ Readability Level Grade Level
90–100 Very Easy 5th grade
70–89 Easy 6th–8th grade
60–69 Plain English High school
50–59 Fairly Difficult College
30–49 Difficult University
0–29 Very Confusing Graduate level
